Rumbalpuy Dhäwu
Stories about the body

Version: 1.0.7

This app is an adaptation of the bilingual print dictionary, “Dictionary of Anatomy: Dhäruk Mala ga Mayali' Rumbalpuy", (ARDS Inc., 2009). This app edition contains audio, some revisions of the original content and some translations from Dhuwal into Dhuwala and Dhaŋu. When the print dictionary was published, it was the first of its kind in an Aboriginal language. For many Yolŋu users, the information was new. We hope that this audio app will make this information even more accessible.

The app has two main aims:

  1. The first is to make Western understandings about health and medical terminology available in Yolŋu languages. The app is not a substitute for an interpreter working with a medical professional, but it is a start to being able to explain terminology and Western conceptualisations of the body in language that makes sense to Yolŋu.
  2. The second aim is to support maintenance of Yolŋu languages. This was a deliberate choice made by the translators. Where possible they have used specialised Yolŋu terminology to translate the specialised English terminology. The aim is to support continued use of these Yolŋu terms, many of which are not everyday terms. In other instances, the Yolŋu terms are coinages, just as they were coined originally in English. For example, DNA is translated as djinaga'puy wäyuk, meaning ‘law of the inside’ or more literally ‘upper arm of the inside’. These translation choices were made in order to help Yolŋu languages to stay vibrant through use in all areas of life.

Issues of pragmatics: It is important to note that Yolŋu have different ways of talking about health issues, not just different words. In the Western medical sphere, many professionals value directness and discussion of risk. In contrast, many Yolŋu will choose to galipum waŋa, or 'talk circuitously' when talking about a sensitive topics, for example cancer, women’s health, certain digestive functions. This is in order not to ŋayaŋu-wutthun, 'hit the inner being' of the ailing person. It should be stressed that some Yolŋu do prefer a direct approach when it comes to medical information. In any case, users should be aware that some entries are not told in the most sensitive way for many Yolŋu, despite having been translated into the languages.

About the languages used in this app: The names Dhuwal, Dhaŋu and Dhuwala are terms used to identify groups of Yolŋu clan languages by their respective terms for 'this'. Not all of the Yolŋu languages are represented in this app. This app uses the clan languages of Djambarrpuyŋu (Dhuwal), Gälpu (Dhaŋu) and Gumatj (Dhuwala). We acknowledge that there are many, many more Indigenous languages across Australia that are not yet available on this app. We hope that English-speaking users from many backgrounds can also benefit from the Plain English explanations of the medical terminology available here.

Choosing languages in this app: Most Yolŋu are multilingual. If you are trying to help someone use this app and you are not sure which language to choose, just let them know that there is information available in Dhuwal, Dhuwala and Dhaŋu, and they'll tell you which language would suit them best. It would also help to advise them that not all the entries are translated into Dhuwala and Dhaŋu yet, and that the app will play the Dhuwal if there is no Dhuwala or Dhaŋu translation available.

The app is fully functional offline: When connected to the internet, the latest updates will be synchronised with your phone, using only a small amount of data.

Future directions: We value your feedback towards continually refining and growing this app. Subject to funding, ARDS hopes to complete the translation of the dictionary into Dhaŋu and Dhuwala, to record English audio, to include other languages, to include direct and alternative ways to discuss sensitive topics, and to include more entries.

WARNING: This may contain audio of deceased people. Please notify us if you would like to have such material removed.

The information provided in this app is provided to help Yolŋu better access Balanda medical knowledge and to support effective communication between medical professionals, interpreters and patients. It is not a substitute for a medical professional working with a qualified interpreter.

You should not act on the basis of anything contained in this app without seeking independent professional medical advice. If you have any concerns about your health, consult your general practitioner.
